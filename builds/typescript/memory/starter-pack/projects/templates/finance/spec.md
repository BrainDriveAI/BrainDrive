## What makes this spec good (the "hill to climb")

A good Finance spec passes these tests:

- **User stories use the owner's exact words** — quote, don't paraphrase. The owner reads them and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want and why."
- **Where You Are has 3+ concrete data points** — numbers, not narratives. Income, debt, savings, employer benefits — actual values.
- **Specificity rewarded.** "$220/month in interest" beats "high interest rates." "$14K at 22% APR" beats "credit card debt."
- **Obstacles are specific** — "we fight every Sunday about chores" not "communication issues." "Lifestyle inflation since the raise" not "spending too much."
- **Gaps separated by impact** — "before the plan is complete" (could change direction) vs "worth exploring later" (interesting but won't shift today's plan).

## Interview methodology — How to fill this spec

When the spec is `Pre-interview` or `Partial`, run an interview to fill it. This methodology applies broadly across all domains, with finance-specific guidance below.

### Universal interview rules

- **One question at a time.** Listen to the answer. Follow up on what matters. Do NOT bundle 3+ questions in one message — that's a form, not a conversation.
- **Landscape first, specifics second.** The owner usually comes with a specific concern. That's the entry point, not the starting point. Build the full picture first, then circle back to their specific question with real context.
- **Never accept vague answers.** "I want to get healthier" isn't enough. A good advisor probes until it's specific.
- **Confirm as you go.** As you gather enough for each major section, play it back naturally and get confirmation. "Here's what I'm hearing about your finances: [summary]. Sound right?" Focus confirmation on high-stakes sections: What You Want, Where You Are, What's In The Way.
- **Confirm user stories before writing the plan.** Play them back: "Here's what I'm hearing you want — [stories]. Does this capture it?" The plan is built to serve these stories. If stories are wrong, plan solves the wrong problem.
- **~5 minutes is the target.** Adapt — detailed answers get there fast; vague answers need more probing.
- **The user stories in this spec are the most important output.** They should be specific enough that the owner reads them and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want and why."

### Finance-specific interview context

**Start with the full financial picture.** The owner usually comes with a specific concern — debt, saving, investing, a big purchase. Before diving in, map: income, expenses, debt, savings, investments, employer benefits. The specific concern gets much better advice with the whole picture.

**This domain rewards specificity.** Show the math. "$12K at 22% APR is costing you $220/month in interest alone." Vague financial advice is useless financial advice.

**Common blind spots to surface:**
- Partner alignment on money — if they mention a partner, this is often the biggest unlock
- Employer benefits left on the table (401K match, HSA, stock options)
- Emotional avoidance of the real numbers — "I don't want to look" usually means anxiety of not knowing is worse than reality. When stress eating / avoidance purchases surface, probe one level deeper before moving on.
- Lifestyle inflation — income went up, savings didn't

**Common interview branches:**
- Debt crisis → focus on payoff strategy, interest math, spending control
- Growth/investing → focus on goals, risk tolerance, timeline
- Life transition (new job, divorce, baby, house) → focus on decisions that can't wait. Must-ask: health insurance status, tax filing changes, time-sensitive settlement deadlines. When a divorce settlement includes retirement accounts, frame the potential magnitude.
- General anxiety → focus on making the invisible visible (where does the money actually go?)

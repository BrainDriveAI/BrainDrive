## What makes this spec good (the "hill to climb")

A good Relationships spec passes these tests:

- **User stories use the owner's exact words** — quote, don't paraphrase. The owner reads them and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want and why."
- **Where You Are is a relationship MAP, not a single conflict.** Key people, how those relationships are going, communication patterns, what's working and what isn't. "My relationship with my kids is great, my marriage is strained, I haven't talked to my sister in a year" — that's the landscape.
- **Concrete behaviors, not vague labels.** "We argue every Sunday about chores and it escalates until someone leaves the room" beats "communication issues."
- **Patterns called out across relationships.** If something keeps happening with different people, the pattern is theirs.
- **Obstacles probe past the surface.** "The dishes" is rarely about the dishes — capture what's underneath.
- **Gaps separated by impact** — "before the plan is complete" (could change direction) vs "worth exploring later."

## Interview methodology — How to fill this spec

When the spec is `Pre-interview` or `Partial`, run an interview to fill it. This methodology applies broadly across all domains, with relationships-specific guidance below.

### Universal interview rules

- **One question at a time.** Listen to the answer. Follow up on what matters. Do NOT bundle 3+ questions in one message — that's a form, not a conversation.
- **Landscape first, specifics second.** People come with a specific situation — a fight, a distance, a frustration. But that situation lives inside a broader landscape. Get the full picture first, then circle back.
- **Never accept vague answers.** Probe gently — relationships need more nuance than other domains.
- **Confirm as you go.** Play back high-stakes sections.
- **Confirm user stories before writing the plan.**
- **~5 minutes is the target.** This domain often runs longer — that's OK. Don't rush.
- **The user stories in this spec are the most important output.**

### Relationships-specific interview context

**Start with the landscape, not the problem.** People usually come with a specific situation — a fight, distance, frustration. Get the full picture first: who are the important people, how are those relationships going generally, what patterns show up across them. Then circle back to whatever brought them here — now it has context.

**This domain requires the most nuance.** Listen more, probe gently, avoid jumping to solutions. Help them find the goal underneath the situation.

**Where You Are is a relationship map, not a single conflict:** Key people, how those relationships are going, communication patterns. "Communication issues" means nothing — "we argue every Sunday about chores and it escalates until someone leaves the room" means something.

**Common blind spots to surface:**
- The surface issue isn't the real one — "the dishes" is rarely about the dishes. Probe for what's underneath.
- Patterns across relationships — if it keeps happening with different people, the pattern is theirs
- Unspoken expectations — things they want but have never said out loud
- Avoidance disguised as patience — "I'm giving them space" sometimes means "I'm avoiding the conversation"
- They may not realize what healthy relationships look like — their baseline might be off

**Common interview branches:**
- Specific conflict → get the landscape first, then focus on the pattern behind the conflict, not the incident
- General dissatisfaction → map all the key relationships, look for what's draining vs energizing
- Growing apart → focus on what changed, what they want it to look like instead
- Family dynamics → focus on boundaries, expectations, and what they can actually control
- "I don't know what's wrong" → the landscape map usually reveals it

### Sensitive topics — escalation, not filing

If the owner reveals abuse, self-harm, or acute mental-health crisis during the interview: **STOP the interview**. Don't try to capture it as a stable fact. Follow `base/AGENT.md` "Sensitive Topics — Escalation, Not Filing":
- Acknowledge directly. Don't deflect.
- Suggest professional support (therapist, crisis line, doctor — be specific where possible).
- **Never pretend to be a therapist. Never diagnose.**
- Don't refuse to engage either.

The interview can resume in a future conversation — only after the owner is grounded and the immediate need is addressed.

## What You Want

Your goals as specific user stories. Each captures what you want to change, what's driving it, and what success looks like. Use the owner's exact words — quote, don't paraphrase.

*Example:*

> *### I want to feel like a team with my partner again*
> *As someone whose relationship has gone flat — we don't fight, but we don't really talk either — I want to reconnect before the distance becomes permanent. I still love him, but I'm scared that if I don't do something, we'll drift into being roommates. Success looks like more nights where I go to bed feeling like "yeah, there he is" — the person I moved in with.*

## Where You Are

Your full relationship landscape — context that shapes each goal above. Partner, family, kids, friends, colleagues — who matters to you and how those relationships are going. Not just the one situation that brought you here — the whole map. What's working, what isn't, and what patterns show up across relationships.

The specific obstacles between you and the goals above. Sometimes obvious (conflict, distance). Sometimes underneath (unspoken expectations, avoidance, patterns that keep repeating). Often the surface issue isn't the real one.

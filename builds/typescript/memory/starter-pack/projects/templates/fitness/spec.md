## What makes this spec good (the "hill to climb")

A good Fitness spec passes these tests:

- **User stories use the owner's exact words** — quote, don't paraphrase. The owner reads them and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want and why."
- **Where You Are reflects truth, not aspiration.** "Walk twice a week, sometimes" beats "I'm pretty active." Most people overstate their baseline — capture what they ACTUALLY do.
- **Constraints are explicit.** Sleep, stress, injuries, schedule, equipment, past attempts — all relevant.
- **Obstacles are specific** — "all-or-nothing thinking — if I can't go 5 days a week why bother" not "no motivation." "Stress eats every Sunday" not "bad diet."
- **Past attempts captured.** Understanding what didn't work is as valuable as knowing what did.
- **Gaps separated by impact** — "before the plan is complete" (could change direction) vs "worth exploring later."

## Interview methodology — How to fill this spec

When the spec is `Pre-interview` or `Partial`, run an interview to fill it. This methodology applies broadly across all domains, with fitness-specific guidance below.

### Universal interview rules

- **One question at a time.** Listen to the answer. Follow up on what matters. Do NOT bundle 3+ questions in one message — that's a form, not a conversation.
- **Landscape first, specifics second.** Owner usually comes with a specific goal — lose weight, get stronger, run a race. That's the entry point, not the starting point. Map the landscape first.
- **Never accept vague answers.** Probe for specifics — what they ACTUALLY do, not what they plan to do.
- **Confirm as you go.** Play back high-stakes sections (What You Want, Where You Are, What's In The Way).
- **Confirm user stories before writing the plan.**
- **~5 minutes is the target.**
- **The user stories in this spec are the most important output.**

### Fitness-specific interview context

**Start with the full health picture.** Goal ≠ starting point. Map: current activity, diet patterns, sleep, stress, injuries, physical constraints, lifestyle. A weight-loss goal looks different when you discover they're sleeping 5 hours and stress-eating — the plan might start with sleep, not exercise.

**This domain has shame and past failure.** Be matter-of-fact about where they are. No judgment, no cheerleading. "You haven't been active in six months — that's our starting point, and it's fine."

**Common blind spots to surface:**
- All-or-nothing thinking — "if I can't go 5 days a week, why bother?" Call this out directly.
- Sleep quality affecting energy and motivation — fitness plans fail when sleep is broken
- Stress eating or skipping meals — nutrition drives results more than exercise
- Event-driven motivation that fades — wedding/vacation goals are fine, but what happens after?

**Common interview branches:**
- Weight loss → focus on sustainable habits, not crash diets. What's their relationship with food?
- Strength/performance → focus on current baseline, realistic progression, injury prevention
- Energy/wellbeing → focus on sleep, stress, movement patterns — may not need a "program" at all
- Return from injury/break → focus on constraints, gradual progression, what's safe. **When the primary blocker is fear of re-injury rather than physical limitation, flag mental health support (sports psychologist, therapist experienced with injury recovery) as a path-changing gap in Phase 1, not a later-stage option.**

## What You Want

Your goals as specific user stories. Each captures what you want to change, what's driving it, and what success looks like. Use the owner's exact words — quote, don't paraphrase.

*Example:*

> *### I want to lose 25 pounds and feel strong*
> *As someone who used to be active but has let it slide, I want to get back to a body I'm comfortable in. I'm tired of feeling sluggish and I want to be the active parent, not the tired one. Success looks like having energy after work, feeling good in my clothes, and exercise being part of my week — not a punishment I dread.*

## Where You Are

Your full health and fitness landscape — context that shapes each goal above. Current activity, diet, sleep, stress, injuries, constraints, and how it fits into your life. Not just the thing you want to change — the whole picture. Not where you wish you were — where you actually are. No judgment, just the baseline.

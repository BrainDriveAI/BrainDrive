## What makes this spec good (the "hill to climb")

A good Career spec passes these tests:

- **User stories use the owner's exact words** — quote, don't paraphrase. The owner reads them and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want and why."
- **Where You Are has concrete data** — role, industry, tenure, income, key skills, satisfaction, trajectory. Where are they thriving? Where stagnating?
- **Reframe-tested narratives.** People come with stories ("I can't switch because…"). The spec captures what they BELIEVE plus what's actually true after probing.
- **Obstacles are specific** — "haven't told manager about wanting promotion" not "career stuck." "Vesting cliff in 14 months" not "timing is hard."
- **Gaps separated by impact** — "before the plan is complete" (could change direction) vs "worth exploring later."

## Interview methodology — How to fill this spec

When the spec is `Pre-interview` or `Partial`, run an interview to fill it. This methodology applies broadly across all domains, with career-specific guidance below.

### Universal interview rules

- **One question at a time.** Listen to the answer. Follow up on what matters. Do NOT bundle 3+ questions in one message — that's a form, not a conversation.
- **Landscape first, specifics second.** The owner usually comes with a specific trigger (bad review, a job listing, feeling stuck). That's the entry point, not the starting point. Build the full picture first, then circle back with real context.
- **Never accept vague answers.** Probe until specific.
- **Confirm as you go.** Play back high-stakes sections (What You Want, Where You Are, What's In The Way) and get confirmation before moving on.
- **Confirm user stories before writing the plan.** "Here's what I'm hearing you want — [stories]. Does this capture it?"
- **~5 minutes is the target.** Adapt to the person.
- **The user stories in this spec are the most important output.**

### Career-specific interview context

**Start with the full career picture.** Trigger ≠ starting point. Map the landscape: current role, industry, tenure, income, skills, satisfaction, trajectory. A question about "should I look for a new job" looks very different when you understand whether they're underpaid, undervalued, or just bored.

**This domain benefits from reframing.** People come with narratives ("I can't switch because…") — your job is to test those narratives, not accept them. Challenge assumptions about what's possible.

**Common blind spots to surface:**
- Impostor syndrome disguised as "exploring options" — sometimes they know exactly what they want but don't think they can get it
- Golden handcuffs — good salary keeping them in a role they've outgrown
- Haven't told anyone — wanting a promotion but never had a career conversation with manager → that's step zero
- Vesting cliffs and timelines unmapped — timing matters for career moves
- Partner impact — career changes affect households, not just individuals

**Common interview branches:**
- Promotion/advancement → focus on gaps between current and target role, key conversations to have
- Career pivot → focus on transferable skills, financial runway, realistic timeline
- Escape (toxic environment, burnout) → focus on what they NEED vs what they're RUNNING from. Burnout cases may involve unprocessed trauma — acknowledge it, flag professional support as a plan prerequisite, not afterthought. If symptoms (nightmares, intrusive memories, guilt about leaving) surface, those aren't career problems and the career plan won't fix them.
- "I don't know what I want" → focus on what energizes them, what drains them, pattern recognition
